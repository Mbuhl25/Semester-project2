Introduction
“If you are curious, you'll find the puzzles around you. If you are determined, you will solve them.” - Erno Rubik [https://www.brainyquote.com/quotes/erno_rubik_952345]

In the year 1974, Erno Rubik, created the rubik’s cube, because he wanted to find a way to teach his students three-dimensional movements [https://www.smithsonianmag.com/innovation/brief-history-rubiks-cube-180975911/]. Now the cube is know across the world for its simple appearance and underlying complexity. It consists of 8 corner pieces, 12 edge pieces and 6 center pieces, and each side of the cube has a different color. The cube allows for rotation around each centerpiece, and by turning the sides, it enters a scrambled state. The cube is solved once all colors are moved to their corresponding sides.
To obtain a sequence of moves to solve the cube, it is necessary to know how the cube is scrambled. This can be done using computer-vision to obtain the color of each of the 54 squares of the cube. Once every side is known, an algorithm called kociemba [https://kociemba.org/index.html] can calculate the algorithm to solve the cube. 
The challenges proposed as a human differs greatly from the challenges a robot faces when solving a rubik’s cube. A human has the advantage of having two hands, which makes it easy to move around the cube and perform rotations on each side of the cube, on the other hand, a human doesn’t have the computational power of a robot to calculate the quickest solving algorithm. Instead a human has to learn the method, which includes preset algorithms to get from step to step [see CFOP method https://jperm.net/3x3/cfop]. Most industrial robots on the other hand, only have one arm to manipulate the objects with. Therefore a gripper solution which has the possibility of rotating each side of the cube has to be developed.
The gripper is a crucial part of the overall project, as it needs to securely hold the cube both while stationary and while rotating the sides of the cube. A human hand has skin that provides friction, making gripping easier. A mechanical gripper must use a material that also creates friction for achieving better grip. This can be achieved by using rubber pads, textured surfaces, or other high friction materials at the contact points.
Human hands are also highly dexterous, with five fingers and 27 joints, allowing complex gripping and manipulation. Replicating this level of dexterity in a mechanical gripper would result in a very complex and impractical design. Therefore, the human hand can instead be used as inspiration, while reducing complexity to achieve a solution that is both effective and mechanically feasible.
Based on these considerations, the gripper design focuses on providing enough contact points to stabilize the cube while still allowing individual faces to rotate. This requires a balance between grip strength and freedom of movement. The gripper must hold the cube firmly enough to prevent slipping. Additionally, the design should allow the robot to reposition the cube when needed, ensuring that all sides can be accessed during the solving process.
research problem
Design a system that is capable of generating an algorithm for solving a rubik’s cube, as well as performing the provided algorithm as rotations on the physical cube, and solve it.
Furthermore, a gripper that can perform given instructions and read execution data should be designed. The system should be able to have communication between both the robot, the gripper and the computer.
The evaluation of the project will be based upon the speed, which the robot solves the rubik’s cube, including tests on the average speed of a rotation of a single side, and the overall average of a solve. A test of precision can be evaluated by measuring how often the robot gripper successfully grabs the side of the cube, and completes a rotation.
